Persuasion effect · Issue One

How Can We Help?

Warning signs to watch for, what may cause a teen to struggle, and how to actually support somebody who is.

By Kaylee · Spring 2026

If you have read the Information Effect, you already know that mental health struggles among teens are common. The next question is the one that actually matters: what do we do about it? This piece is for parents, friends, teachers, coaches, siblings — anyone who has ever watched somebody they love seem to be struggling and not known what to say. There is a way to help. It is smaller and more possible than you think.

Warning signs to watch for

According to Mayo Clinic and Psychiatry.org, the warning signs of a mental health condition in teens are not always dramatic. They show up most often as quiet changes in someone’s usual behavior. The most common ones include:

  • Pulling away from friends, family, or activities they used to love
  • Big changes in sleep — sleeping much more or much less than usual
  • Big changes in eating — appetite loss or eating much more
  • Persistent sadness, irritability, or low mood that lasts more than a couple weeks
  • Trouble concentrating, a sudden drop in grades, or losing interest in school
  • Talking about feeling hopeless, worthless, or like a burden
  • Increased anxiety, panic, or constant worry
  • Talking about death or self-harm — even “jokingly”

One sign on its own does not mean a teen is in crisis. But several of these together, especially if they last for more than a couple weeks, are worth paying attention to.

What may cause a teen to struggle

There is rarely one single cause. Most of the time it is a combination of factors that build up over time:

  • Biological: family history, brain chemistry, hormonal changes during puberty
  • Environmental: bullying, family conflict, unsafe homes, financial stress, the loss of someone close
  • Social: isolation, social media pressure, comparison, online harassment
  • Academic: overload, perfectionism, college pressure, being the “strong one”
  • Identity: figuring out who you are while everyone seems to be watching

You don’t have to fix anything. You have to be present. That is most of the work.

How you can actually help

The most important thing I learned in my research is that you do not need to be a therapist to help a teen who is struggling. You need to be willing to listen. Here is what that actually looks like:

  • Notice and reach out. If something feels off, say so. “Hey, I’ve noticed you’ve seemed quieter lately. Are you okay?” is enough.
  • Listen without trying to fix. Most teens who open up are not asking for advice. They are asking to be heard. Resist the urge to jump to solutions.
  • Take it seriously. Don’t minimize. Don’t say “everyone feels that way sometimes.” Believe them the first time.
  • Stay calm. If a teen tells you they are struggling, your reaction will tell them whether to keep being honest. Calm is the right response.
  • Help them connect with support. A school counselor, a therapist, a parent, a doctor, a crisis line. Offer to go with them, sit with them while they call, or look up resources together.
  • Keep showing up. One conversation is rarely enough. Check in again next week. And the week after that.

If you are reading this and you are the one struggling: please know that you are not a burden. The people in your life who love you would rather be in a hard conversation with you than miss it. And if there is no one safe to talk to right now, the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline is free, confidential, and available 24/7. You can call or text. You don’t need a reason that sounds big enough.
